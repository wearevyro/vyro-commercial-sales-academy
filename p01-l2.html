Lesson 1.2 — Commercial Rights & How Value Flows

1. Lesson Overview

Lesson 1.1 taught you who is in the room. This lesson teaches you what's actually being traded and where the money goes.

"Commercial rights" is the single most important phrase in this entire academy, and it is also the most consistently misunderstood by people entering this industry from a general sales or marketing background. A commercial right is not a logo placement. It is a specific, ownable, licensable piece of value that a Rights Holder controls and can grant, in whole or in part, to someone else in exchange for money or strategic value.

This lesson defines commercial rights precisely, walks through exactly how money and value move through a real deal from first conversation to renewal, and introduces the concept of a commercial relationship as something VYRO manages over years, not something that ends at contract signature.

By the end of Phase 01, you should be able to sit across from anyone — a new hire, a client, a brand's marketing director — and explain in plain language what VYRO does and why it works. This lesson is the second half of that foundation.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  1. Define "commercial rights" precisely and distinguish a right from an asset and from an activation.
  2. Name the major categories of commercial rights and give a real example of each.
  3. Walk through, step by step, how a dollar moves from a Brand's marketing budget to a Rights Holder's bank account through a VYRO-managed deal — including where VYRO's fee is taken.
  4. Explain the difference between a transactional sponsorship sale and a managed commercial relationship, and why VYRO is built around the latter.
  5. Diagram the complete money flow for the university client scenario introduced in Lesson 1.1, correctly placing VYRO's revenue mechanism (fee, commission, or revenue share) in the flow.

3. Business Context

A rights holder doesn't sell "a sponsorship." It sells a bundle of specific, definable rights — the use of its logo, a sign on its scoreboard, a segment on its broadcast, a booth at its event, access to its email list, category exclusivity in a defined market. Every one of those is a distinct commercial right with its own value, its own negotiation, and its own contractual language.

This distinction matters commercially because it is the difference between a rights holder who can only say "we have sponsorship opportunities" — vague, unpriceable, hard to sell — and a rights holder VYRO has properly onboarded, who can say "we have eleven distinct commercial rights across five categories, each individually valued and packageable." The second version is what VYRO builds for every client, and it's the reason a VYRO-represented rights holder consistently sells more, and sells it faster, than one trying to sell itself.

Money flow matters for the same reason it matters in any business: if a new employee can't explain where the money comes from, where it goes, and where VYRO's revenue sits in that chain, they cannot credibly negotiate a deal, structure a contract, or explain VYRO's value to a skeptical client or prospect.

4. Why This Matters

Three consequences of not understanding this material cold:

  • You will underprice or overpackage inventory because you're treating a bundle of distinct rights as one vague "sponsorship," instead of pricing each right on its own merits and bundling deliberately (this becomes critical in Phase 7: Valuation and Phase 8: Packaging).
  • You will not be able to explain VYRO's fee structure with confidence in a client conversation — and hesitation on this specific topic is the fastest way to lose a rights holder's trust during a pitch to become their commercial rights company.
  • You will confuse a one-time sale with a relationship, and undersell VYRO's actual value proposition, which is long-term commercial management, not a single transaction. Rights holders that view VYRO as a one-time vendor churn; rights holders that understand VYRO as their commercial department renew for years.

5. Core Lesson

5.1 What Is a Commercial Right?

A commercial right is a specific, definable piece of value that a Rights Holder legally controls and can grant — exclusively or non-exclusively, temporarily or long-term — to another party in exchange for compensation.

Three things make something a commercial right rather than just "an idea for a sponsorship":

  1. It's ownable. The Rights Holder can point to exactly what they control (a physical space, a media segment, a data set, a piece of intellectual property, a name).
  2. It's grantable. It can be licensed, leased, or assigned to someone else without VYRO's client giving up ownership of the underlying asset.
  3. It's boundable. It has defined terms — duration, exclusivity, territory, category — that can be written into a contract.

"We'll get you some visibility" is not a commercial right. "Category-exclusive presenting sponsorship of the men's basketball program, including logo on court signage, 200 in-broadcast mentions per season, and first right of refusal on jersey patch rights beginning 2027" is a commercial right — or more precisely, a bundle of several.

5.2 Major Categories of Commercial Rights

  • Naming Rights — the right to attach a brand name to a venue, facility, or program (a stadium, an arena, a training facility).
  • Media Rights — the right to broadcast, stream, or otherwise distribute coverage of events or content.
  • Sponsorship / Partnership Rights — category-based rights granting a brand association with the property at a defined tier (Title, Presenting, Official Partner, Supplier — covered fully in Phase 2).
  • Signage & Venue Rights — physical or digital branding within a venue (scoreboard, concourse, court/field signage).
  • Digital & Social Rights — branded content, mentions, or integrations across the Rights Holder's owned digital and social channels.
  • Hospitality & Premium Rights — suites, premium seating, VIP experiences, access-based inventory.
  • Licensing Rights — the right to produce or sell merchandise using the Rights Holder's marks.
  • Data & CRM Rights — access to fan or attendee data for marketing purposes (handled with strict compliance boundaries — see Phase 6).

Every one of these is independently valuable, independently priceable, and independently negotiable. Packaging (Phase 8) is the skill of bundling the right combination of these for a specific brand's objective — but you cannot package intelligently until you can list these categories without hesitation.

5.3 How Money Flows Through a VYRO-Managed Deal

Walk this through in order, using the Lesson 1.1 university scenario:

  1. The Rights Holder engages VYRO as its commercial rights company, typically under a management agreement defining VYRO's scope (full commercial program, or specific categories) and compensation structure.
  2. VYRO audits and inventories the Rights Holder's commercial assets (Phase 6) and develops a valuation (Phase 7) and packaged offering (Phase 8) — this is cost and time VYRO invests before any revenue is generated.
  3. VYRO sells commercial rights to Brands, negotiating and contracting each partnership on the Rights Holder's behalf.
  4. The Brand pays a rights fee, typically to VYRO (as the Rights Holder's authorized commercial representative) or, in some structures, directly to the Rights Holder with VYRO invoicing separately.
  5. VYRO takes its compensation — structured as one of three common models (a flat management fee, a sales commission on rights fees generated, or a revenue-share arrangement) — and remits the remainder to the Rights Holder.
  6. The Rights Holder receives new, incremental commercial revenue it would not have generated on its own, net of VYRO's compensation.
  7. VYRO manages activation, reporting, and renewal (Phases 9 and 10) to protect and grow the relationship, because a renewed and upsold partnership is worth significantly more over time than a single-year sale.

The critical point for a new employee: VYRO's compensation is earned by generating revenue the Rights Holder could not generate alone — not by taking a cut of revenue that would have existed anyway. This is the honest, correct way to explain VYRO's fee to a skeptical client, and you should be able to say it in exactly those terms without notes.

5.4 Transactional Sale vs. Managed Commercial Relationship

A transactional sponsorship broker finds a buyer, closes a deal, collects a placement fee, and moves on. VYRO is built around the opposite model: a managed commercial relationship that persists across years, through activation, reporting, renewal, and expansion.

This matters because the actual lifetime value of a commercial partnership is realized in years two through five, not year one — through renewals, upsells, and reduced acquisition cost (a renewed sponsor doesn't require a new prospecting and discovery cycle). Every phase from Phase 9 onward exists because VYRO treats the relationship, not the transaction, as the product.

6. Commercial Frameworks

Framework: Right → Value → Package → Price

Every commercial right moves through this sequence before it reaches a brand:

COMMERCIAL RIGHT (what is legally ownable/grantable)
        ↓
VALUE (what is it worth, based on reach, exclusivity, scarcity — Phase 7)
        ↓
PACKAGE (which rights bundle together for a specific brand objective — Phase 8)
        ↓
PRICE (the number attached to the package, defensible against the value analysis)

New employees who skip straight to "price" without doing the first three steps are the reason inexperienced sales teams underprice inventory.

Framework: The Money Flow Ledger

For any deal, be able to fill in every line:

Line Who Amount / Mechanism
Brand pays Brand → VYRO (or Brand → Rights Holder, VYRO invoices separately) Rights fee, per contract
VYRO compensation Deducted per management agreement Flat fee / commission % / revenue share %
Rights Holder receives Net of VYRO's compensation New incremental commercial revenue
Reinvestment (optional) Rights Holder or VYRO Activation budget, if separately funded

If you cannot fill in this table for a deal you're discussing, you do not yet understand the deal.

7. Step-by-Step Process

How to explain VYRO's money flow to a skeptical prospect, in order:

  1. State plainly what VYRO does: "We become your outsourced commercial department."
  2. Explain the investment VYRO makes first: audit, valuation, and packaging — before any revenue exists.
  3. Explain that VYRO sells the packaged rights to brands and collects the rights fee.
  4. State VYRO's compensation model plainly and without hedging (fee, commission, or revenue share — whichever applies).
  5. State the net outcome for the Rights Holder: new revenue they were not capturing before, minus VYRO's compensation, which VYRO only earns by generating that revenue.
  6. Pivot to the relationship, not the transaction: explain that VYRO's job continues after signature — activation, reporting, renewal — because that's where the compounding value is.

How to diagram money flow for a new prospect or case study:

  1. Identify the Brand(s) and what they're paying for (which specific rights).
  2. Identify who the Brand pays — VYRO directly, or the Rights Holder with VYRO invoicing.
  3. Identify VYRO's compensation mechanism, if known or reasonably inferable (fee/commission/revenue share).
  4. Identify what the Rights Holder nets after VYRO's compensation.
  5. Identify whether any revenue is earmarked for activation versus pure revenue to the Rights Holder.

8. Real Industry Examples

IMG College Learfield's rights-fee model with university athletic departments — a widely documented structure in which the commercial rights company pays a guaranteed rights fee to the university (sometimes with revenue-share upside) in exchange for the exclusive right to sell and manage the school's multimedia and sponsorship rights. This shows students a variant money-flow model: instead of a pure commission, the rights company sometimes guarantees the Rights Holder a fee regardless of how much is ultimately sold, absorbing more of the risk itself.

Naming rights deals (e.g., a corporate name attached to an arena or stadium) — these are structured as long-term rights fees paid over the life of the agreement (often 10–20+ years), illustrating that a single commercial right (naming rights) can represent the single largest revenue line in an entire commercial program, dwarfing individual sponsorship categories.

Formula 1 team title partnerships — team sponsors pay rights fees that fund a meaningful share of a team's operating budget, showing money flow at the extreme end: commercial rights revenue isn't a side revenue stream for some rights holders, it is the primary operating budget.

Multi-year league media rights deals (e.g., major U.S. sports leagues' broadcast agreements) — demonstrate media rights as their own massive, separately negotiated category of commercial right, distinct from sponsorship, and show how media rights value increases the value of every sponsorship sold around that inventory (more cameras, more mentions, higher sponsorship value).

9. VYRO Best Practices

  • Always separate "the right" from "the price." Rights holders and brands negotiate more productively when the conversation starts with precisely what is being granted, not with a number.
  • Be able to state VYRO's compensation model out loud, plainly, in a client conversation, without hesitation. Hesitation reads as something being hidden, even when nothing is.
  • Frame VYRO's fee as earned, not extracted. VYRO is compensated for generating revenue the client could not generate independently — this is true, and it is also the correct sales narrative.
  • Never present a single-year deal as the finish line internally. Every deal should be discussed, from day one, in terms of its multi-year relationship potential.
  • When mapping money flow for a new prospect, always ask directly (in discovery) how their existing commercial rights, if any, are currently structured — flat fee, commission, revenue share, or fully in-house — because this shapes what a VYRO engagement would look like and how to position it.

10. Common Mistakes

  • Treating "commercial rights" and "sponsorship" as synonyms. Sponsorship is one category of commercial right; naming rights, media rights, licensing, and data rights are others.
  • Presenting price before value. Skipping the Right → Value → Package → Price sequence produces prices that collapse under negotiation because they were never defensible.
  • Being unable to explain VYRO's compensation model plainly. This is the single fastest credibility loss with a new prospective client — evasiveness on money flow reads as untrustworthy even when the model is completely standard for the industry.
  • Underselling the relationship. New employees, eager to close, sometimes pitch VYRO as a one-time sales service rather than a long-term commercial partner — this undermines the entire business model and the client relationship's long-term value.
  • Confusing gross rights fees with what the Rights Holder actually nets. Always know both numbers, and know the difference is VYRO's earned compensation, not a hidden cost.

11. Professional Tips

  • Practice explaining money flow at three altitudes: a 10-second version ("we generate new commercial revenue for our clients and are compensated for the revenue we generate"), a 60-second version (the six-step flow in Section 5.3), and a full walkthrough with a ledger (Section 6).
  • When studying any partnership announcement, try to infer the money flow even when it isn't disclosed — this is a skill, not just information recall, and it's tested heavily in Phase 3.
  • Keep a mental (or literal) running list of which commercial right categories a given client has sold, and which remain unsold — this becomes the backbone of your packaging work in Phase 8.

16. Manager Coaching Notes

Purpose of this lesson in the employee's development: Lesson 1.1 tested whether the employee can see the ecosystem. This lesson tests whether they understand the economics well enough to defend VYRO's business model under mild pushback — a skill they will need in nearly every early client and prospect conversation.

Discussion questions to ask in review:

  • "A prospective client says, 'why wouldn't we just hire our own sales person instead of paying VYRO?' How do you respond?" (Listen for value-creation framing, not defensiveness.)
  • "Walk me through where the money goes on a deal you researched for your homework — all the way from the brand's marketing budget to the rights holder's bank account."
  • "What's the difference between a commercial right and a sponsorship package?" (Tests Right → Value → Package → Price sequencing.)

Expected answers: A proficient employee can state VYRO's value proposition and compensation model without hedging, correctly sequences right-value-package-price, and can reconstruct money flow for an unfamiliar deal with reasonable accuracy.

Coaching tips:

  • If an employee is vague or uncomfortable discussing VYRO's fee structure, role-play the skeptical-client scenario repeatedly until the answer is fluent and confident — this is a rehearsed skill, not an innate one.
  • If an employee treats every deal as a one-time transaction in conversation, redirect them to describe the multi-year relationship value explicitly, every time, until it becomes automatic.
  • Watch for employees who can recite the eight rights categories but can't apply them to a real announcement — application is the actual bar, not recall.

Common employee mistakes to watch for:

  • Hedging or vague language when asked directly how VYRO gets paid.
  • Skipping straight to price when discussing a hypothetical deal, without first identifying the specific rights and their value.
  • Describing the Rights Holder's revenue as "commission we take from them" instead of correctly framing VYRO's compensation as tied to incremental revenue generated.

Evaluation checklist: see Grading Rubric (Section 21).

18. Practical Exercise

Exercise: Defend the Fee

Manager presents the employee with a brief, realistic client objection in writing (e.g., "Your commission feels high — why shouldn't we just do this ourselves?"). Employee has 15 minutes to write a response memo defending VYRO's compensation model, using the "earned, not extracted" framing and at least one concrete reason a Rights Holder cannot easily replicate VYRO's results internally (expertise, relationships, valuation methodology, dedicated bandwidth).

Evaluation focus: confidence, accuracy, and whether the response reframes the objection around value rather than becoming defensive.

19. Role-Play Exercise

Scenario: You are a VYRO account executive in a renewal conversation. Your manager plays a Rights Holder client whose commercial revenue grew significantly in year one, and who is now questioning whether they still need VYRO for year two.

Manager's role-play brief: Open with, "Honestly, now that we have these relationships, couldn't we just manage this ourselves next year?" Push back at least once on any answer that doesn't specifically address activation, renewal management, or pipeline development for new categories not yet sold.

What "good" looks like: The employee doesn't just defend past performance — they pivot to forward-looking value: unsold categories still on the table, activation and reporting work required to protect existing partnerships, and the compounding cost of a Rights Holder rebuilding brand relationships and valuation expertise from scratch.

23. Key Takeaways

  • A commercial right is ownable, grantable, and boundable — not a vague "opportunity."
  • There are (at minimum) eight major categories of commercial rights: naming, media, sponsorship/partnership, signage/venue, digital/social, hospitality/premium, licensing, and data/CRM.
  • Money flows from Brand → VYRO/Rights Holder → VYRO's compensation is deducted → Rights Holder nets new revenue it could not have generated alone.
  • Always sequence Right → Value → Package → Price. Never start with price.
  • VYRO is a managed commercial relationship, not a transactional broker — this is both the honest description of the business and the correct way to sell it internally and externally.
