Lesson 7.1 — Audience & Reach Valuation Inputs

1. Lesson Overview

Phase 06 taught you to build a complete, catalogued asset inventory. This phase teaches you to put a real, defensible number on it — because an inventory without valuation is just a list, not a sellable product.

Sponsorship valuation is not a single formula you plug numbers into and get a guaranteed-correct answer. It's a disciplined, defensible process combining objective measurement (this lesson) with market judgment (Lesson 7.2). This lesson covers the objective, measurable inputs every valuation starts with: audience size, attendance, broadcast reach, social reach, fan engagement, and media value. Lesson 7.2 covers the market-based and qualitative factors — comparable deals, scarcity, brand fit, category demand, and exclusivity — that turn these raw inputs into an actual, market-tested price.

By the end of this phase, you will value five real assets and be able to defend every number with a stated, explicit methodology — because in real VYRO work, a valuation without a defensible methodology behind it is not a valuation, it's a guess.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  1. Define and correctly gather Audience Size, Attendance, Broadcast Reach, Social Reach, Fan Engagement, and Media Value data for a real commercial asset.
  2. Explain why raw reach numbers alone are insufficient for valuation without an engagement-quality adjustment.
  3. Apply a media value equivalency method to translate exposure into a directional dollar estimate, while correctly characterizing its limitations.
  4. Distinguish which valuation inputs are most relevant to which asset types (e.g., attendance matters more for in-venue signage than for a podcast sponsorship).
  5. Assemble a complete Valuation Input Stack for a real asset, in preparation for Lesson 7.2's market-based pricing.

3. Business Context

When VYRO prices a commercial asset for a client, the number has to survive scrutiny from two directions: the rights holder (who wants to know the price reflects real value, not an arbitrary guess) and the brand (who wants to know the price is justified relative to alternative marketing spend, per Phase 05). Valuation inputs are the evidence base that makes a price defensible to both audiences. An employee who cannot explain exactly where a number came from — what data, what method, what assumption — cannot credibly negotiate that number when challenged (Phase 04's negotiation skills depend directly on this).

4. Why This Matters

  • A price without a stated methodology cannot be defended in negotiation. When a brand pushes back on price (Phase 04), the response has to reference specific inputs, not vague confidence.
  • Raw numbers alone can mislead. A large but disengaged audience is worth less than a smaller, highly engaged one — valuation has to account for quality, not just quantity.
  • Media value equivalency is a widely used but imperfect industry technique — VYRO employees need to use it credibly while being honest about its limitations, not present it as more precise than it actually is.

5. Core Lesson

5.1 Audience Size

The total addressable audience across every channel combined — attendees, broadcast viewers, social followers, subscribers, email list size. This is the top-line number establishing the overall scale of an opportunity, but it should never be used alone in a valuation — it's the starting point for the more specific inputs that follow.

5.2 Attendance

The in-person audience specifically — per-event attendance and season/annual aggregate attendance. Attendance matters most for in-venue assets (signage, hospitality, in-venue activation) and matters far less for assets like a podcast sponsorship or a digital-only campaign, where a different input (social/broadcast reach) is more relevant. Always gather both per-event and aggregate figures — a per-event number matters for in-venue signage seen at every game; an aggregate number matters for a season-long sponsorship's total exposure claim.

5.3 Broadcast Reach

Television and streaming viewership figures, distinct from attendance — this is the audience reached through media coverage rather than physical presence. Broadcast reach is the primary input for assets tied to media exposure (in-broadcast signage, broadcast mentions, media rights-adjacent sponsorship elements).

5.4 Social Reach

Total followers, impressions, and reach across a rights holder's social platforms — gathered platform by platform, consistent with Phase 06's Asset Audit approach to social media as a multi-platform, not single, asset. Social reach is the primary input for digital and social sponsorship assets.

5.5 Fan Engagement

A quality-of-audience measure (introduced in Phase 02) — interaction rates, time spent, repeat attendance — that should modify, not simply add to, raw reach numbers. Two properties with identical audience size can have meaningfully different value if one has a highly engaged, active fanbase and the other has a large but passive one. Always gather and report engagement data alongside raw reach, never reach alone.

5.6 Media Value

The practice of translating exposure (signage visibility, broadcast mentions, social impressions) into an estimated advertising-equivalent dollar value — essentially, "what would it cost to buy this same amount of exposure through traditional paid advertising?" This is a widely used industry technique for building a directional value estimate, but it has real limitations: it estimates exposure value, not necessarily the deeper brand-positioning or experiential value sponsorship uniquely provides (Phase 05), and different practitioners can produce different estimates using different assumptions. Always present media value estimates as directional and methodology-transparent, never as a single precise, unchallengeable number.

7. Step-by-Step Process

How to build a Valuation Input Stack for a real asset:

  1. Identify the asset type and determine which inputs are most relevant (Section 6's Input Relevance table).
  2. Gather Audience Size as a top-line reference figure.
  3. Gather the specific relevant input(s): Attendance, Broadcast Reach, and/or Social Reach, both per-event and aggregate where applicable.
  4. Gather Fan Engagement data and apply it as a qualitative modifier to the raw reach figures — note explicitly whether engagement is above, at, or below typical benchmarks for a property of this type.
  5. Calculate a directional Media Value estimate using a stated, transparent equivalency method — document every assumption made.
  6. Compile the complete Valuation Input Stack, ready to feed into Lesson 7.2's market-based pricing process.

8. Real Industry Examples

A property with a large broadcast audience but comparatively modest social engagement, versus a property with a smaller broadcast audience but highly active, engaged social channels — illustrates why Fan Engagement must modify raw reach numbers; a naive valuation based on broadcast reach alone would miss real value differences between these two properties.

A jersey sponsorship valued primarily through broadcast reach and media value equivalency, since the asset is seen consistently across every televised game — illustrates the Input Relevance table in practice: broadcast reach is the dominant input for this specific asset type.

A hospitality/suite package valued primarily through attendance and engagement data, with broadcast reach playing little to no role — illustrates the same principle from the opposite direction: some assets are genuinely reach-independent, and forcing broadcast data into their valuation would be a methodology error.

A media value estimate for a signage package built using a stated equivalency method (e.g., comparing signage exposure time to equivalent broadcast advertising spot pricing) with assumptions clearly documented — illustrates the correct, transparent way to use media value equivalency, versus presenting an unexplained single number as if it were precise fact.

9. VYRO Best Practices

  • Never use audience size alone as a valuation input — always pair it with the more specific, asset-relevant inputs and an engagement modifier.
  • Always select inputs based on genuine relevance to the specific asset type, not out of habit or convenience.
  • Document every assumption behind a media value estimate explicitly — this is what makes the number defensible under negotiation pushback (Phase 04).
  • Present media value estimates as directional, using language like "an estimated equivalency of approximately X, based on Y assumptions" rather than presenting a bare number as precise fact.
  • Gather engagement data with the same rigor as reach data — a valuation built on reach alone is incomplete by VYRO's standard.

10. Common Mistakes

  • Using audience size as if it were sufficient on its own, without asset-specific inputs or an engagement modifier.
  • Gathering broadcast reach data for assets where it's largely irrelevant (e.g., a hospitality package), wasting effort and diluting the valuation's focus.
  • Presenting a media value estimate as a precise, unchallengeable number rather than a transparent, assumption-based directional estimate.
  • Ignoring fan engagement entirely and valuing two properties with similar raw reach as equivalent, missing real quality differences.
  • Failing to document assumptions, making the final number impossible to defend when challenged in negotiation.

11. Professional Tips

  • Build the habit of asking "which inputs actually matter for this specific asset?" before gathering any data — this prevents wasted effort and keeps the valuation focused.
  • Keep a personal reference of typical engagement benchmarks across property types you've researched — this makes it faster to judge whether a new property's engagement is above or below typical for comparison purposes.
  • Always write your media value equivalency assumptions down as you make them, in real time — reconstructing assumptions after the fact is unreliable and undermines defensibility.

16. Manager Coaching Notes

Purpose of this lesson in the employee's development: This lesson tests methodological discipline — whether the employee gathers the right inputs for the right reasons, and whether they can articulate assumptions clearly enough for someone else to follow and challenge their reasoning. Managers should focus review specifically on assumption transparency, since this is what determines whether a valuation is negotiation-ready later in Phase 07 and Phase 04.

Discussion questions to ask in review:

  • "Walk me through your Media Value assumptions for the Jersey Sponsorship asset — if I challenged one of them, could you defend it?"
  • "Why did you choose the inputs you chose for the hospitality-adjacent asset in your Input Relevance worksheet, and why did you exclude others?"
  • "Where did Fan Engagement change your read on an asset's value compared to what raw reach alone would have suggested?"

Expected answers: A proficient employee can defend every assumption specifically (not vaguely), correctly justifies input selection by asset type, and can point to a concrete example where engagement data changed their assessment.

Coaching tips:

  • If Media Value assumptions are missing or vague, send the homework back — this is a non-negotiable documentation standard given how directly it feeds negotiation defensibility.
  • If an employee gathers the same full input set for every asset regardless of type, walk through the Input Relevance table together until asset-specific selection becomes automatic.

Common employee mistakes to watch for:

  • Gathering irrelevant inputs for a given asset type out of habit.
  • Presenting media value estimates without documented assumptions.
  • Ignoring or underweighting fan engagement data.

Evaluation checklist: see Grading Rubric (Section 20).

18. Practical Exercise

Exercise: Rapid Input Stack

Manager assigns one unfamiliar asset type. Employee has 20 minutes to identify the most relevant inputs, gather or reasonably estimate real or plausible figures, and produce a complete Valuation Input Stack with documented Media Value assumptions.

Evaluation focus: correct input selection for the assigned asset type, and clarity/completeness of documented assumptions.

19. Role-Play Exercise

Scenario: You're presenting a Media Value estimate to a rights holder client who questions where the number came from. Your manager plays this client.

Manager's role-play brief: Ask directly, "how did you get to this number — walk me through it," and push back on at least one specific assumption ("why did you use that comparison for broadcast advertising rates?").

What "good" looks like: The employee walks through the methodology and assumptions clearly and specifically, defends the challenged assumption with real reasoning (or credibly acknowledges its directional nature and offers to refine it with more data), and never simply reasserts the number without explanation.

23. Key Takeaways

  • Sponsorship valuation starts with objective, measurable inputs: Audience Size, Attendance, Broadcast Reach, Social Reach, Fan Engagement, and Media Value.
  • Not every input is relevant to every asset type — always select inputs based on genuine relevance, not habit.
  • Fan Engagement modifies raw reach numbers and should never be ignored or treated as optional.
  • Media Value estimates are directional, assumption-based tools, not precise, unchallengeable numbers — document every assumption explicitly.
  • This lesson's Valuation Input Stacks are the foundation Lesson 7.2 builds on to produce final, market-tested pricing.
