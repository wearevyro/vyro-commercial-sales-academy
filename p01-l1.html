Lesson 1.1 — The Commercial Ecosystem: Stakeholder Map

1. Lesson Overview

Before an employee at VYRO writes a single cold email or sits in a single discovery call, they have to be able to answer one question cold: who, specifically, is in the room on a commercial deal — and what does each of them actually want?

Most people who are new to sponsorship sales think there are two parties in a partnership: the team (or event, or university) and the brand. In practice there are usually five to seven parties with real influence over whether a deal happens, what it's worth, and how long it lasts — and VYRO's entire business model exists because most rights holders don't have the internal expertise to manage all of them at once.

This lesson builds the mental map every VYRO employee uses for the rest of their career: the six core roles in the commercial ecosystem, what each one is actually optimizing for, and where VYRO sits in the middle of all of it as the outsourced commercial department.

This is a foundations lesson. Nothing here is optional and nothing here is safe to skip — Lesson 1.2 (money flow) and every lesson in Phases 2 through 16 assume you have this map memorized.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  1. Name and define the six core roles in a commercial partnership ecosystem: Rights Holder, Brand, Commercial Rights Company, Media, Fans, and secondary stakeholders (agencies, broadcasters, venues, ticketing and digital platforms).
  2. Explain what each stakeholder is optimizing for — in their language, not yours.
  3. Identify all stakeholders present in a real, published partnership announcement.
  4. Explain, in one sentence each, why VYRO exists as a category and what problem it solves for a rights holder that the rights holder cannot solve alone.
  5. Correctly distinguish a Rights Holder from a Commercial Rights Company from a Brand when given an unfamiliar organization.

3. Business Context

VYRO is hired by organizations that own something valuable — an audience, a venue, a media platform, an alumni base, a fanbase, intellectual property — but do not have a commercial team capable of turning that asset into revenue at a professional level.

A mid-major university athletic department is a useful mental model to hold throughout this lesson. It has 20 varsity teams, a 15,000-seat football stadium, a basketball arena, a streaming platform, and an alumni network of 80,000 people. It has almost no idea what any of that is worth commercially, no dedicated sales staff, no valuation methodology, and no relationships with the brand and agency world. That gap — between "we own something valuable" and "we can convert it into revenue" — is the entire reason VYRO exists.

Every deal VYRO closes touches multiple stakeholders beyond the rights holder and the brand. If a new employee can't see the full stakeholder map on day one, they will misdiagnose deals for their entire career — they'll pitch the wrong person, price the wrong asset, and lose partnerships they should have won.

4. Why This Matters

If you only understand "team" and "brand," you will make three predictable, expensive mistakes:

  • You'll pitch the rights holder like they're the customer, when in most VYRO deals, the brand is the customer VYRO is ultimately trying to satisfy, and the rights holder is the supplier of the inventory VYRO is monetizing on their behalf.
  • You'll ignore the agency or media partner sitting between you and the actual budget-holder, and wonder why a "perfect fit" brand never responds.
  • You'll undervalue or overvalue an asset because you didn't account for who else is extracting value from the same audience — broadcasters, ticketing platforms, and digital channels all compete for a piece of the same commercial pie.

Elite commercial professionals don't sell to "a company." They map the ecosystem first, then decide who to approach, in what order, and with what message. This lesson is that skill in its earliest form.

5. Core Lesson

5.1 The Six Core Roles

Rights Holder — The organization that owns the underlying asset: the team, university, league, venue, festival, or creator. They own the audience, the brand equity, the venue, and the intellectual property. They are asset-rich and, in the overwhelming majority of VYRO's client base, commercially under-resourced. Examples: a university athletic department, an NCAA conference, a motorsports series, a music festival, a convention center.

Brand — The company investing marketing dollars to reach the rights holder's audience. A brand is not sponsoring a team because it likes the team — it is solving a business problem: awareness, customer acquisition, employee engagement, market entry, category positioning, or community trust. Every brand decision in this space is a marketing budget decision made against other marketing budget decisions (paid social, TV, influencer, retail). Sponsorship has to outperform those alternatives to win the budget.

Commercial Rights Company — This is VYRO's category. A Commercial Rights Company is hired by the Rights Holder to manage some or all of its commercial business: strategy, inventory development, valuation, sales, activation, and reporting. Other companies in this category include Learfield, Legends, Oak View Group, and IMG. What separates a true Commercial Rights Company from a traditional sponsorship broker is scope: VYRO doesn't just introduce brands to rights holders and take a placement fee — VYRO becomes the client's outsourced commercial department, often managing the full lifecycle from asset audit through renewal.

Media — Broadcasters, streaming platforms, and media companies that carry coverage of the rights holder's events or content. Media matters commercially in two ways: it is itself a sellable asset (broadcast and streaming rights are commercial inventory), and it is a value driver for every other asset (a sponsorship is worth more when it comes with guaranteed media exposure).

Fans — The audience being reached. Fans are not a stakeholder in the sense that they sit in negotiations, but they are the reason any of this has value at all. Every valuation, every package, every activation idea ultimately has to answer: does this reach fans in a way that changes their behavior toward the brand? A new employee should always be able to describe who the fans are — demographics, size, engagement level, channels — because that description is the product VYRO is selling.

Secondary stakeholders — Agencies (representing either the brand's marketing budget or the rights holder's talent), broadcasters, venues, ticketing platforms, and digital/social platforms. These parties don't always sit at the negotiating table, but they routinely control a piece of the deal: an agency may control the brand relationship entirely; a venue may own certain signage or naming rights independent of the team that plays there; a ticketing platform may own the attendance data VYRO needs for valuation.

5.2 Where VYRO Sits

VYRO sits structurally between the Rights Holder and the Brand, but functionally VYRO's job is to represent the Rights Holder's commercial interests while building offers the Brand actually wants to buy. This is a dual-fluency job: you have to speak "asset owner" (what does the university actually own, and how much is it worth) and "marketer" (what business problem is this brand trying to solve, and does our inventory solve it).

New employees often assume their job is "sell what the rights holder has." It is closer to: translate what the rights holder has into what the brand needs, then build the deal that gets both sides what they came for.

6. Commercial Frameworks

Framework: The Six-Party Ecosystem Map

For any new prospect or case study, draw this out before doing anything else:

        MEDIA ──────────┐
                         │
RIGHTS HOLDER ──── VYRO ─── BRAND
   │                          │
 FANS                     AGENCIES
   │
VENUES / TICKETING / DIGITAL PLATFORMS

For every box, answer three questions: (1) What do they own or control? (2) What are they optimizing for? (3) Do they have to say yes for this deal to happen?

Framework: "Owns vs. Wants"

A fast diagnostic for any new organization you're studying:

Question Rights Holder Brand
What do they own? Audience, venue, IP, media rights Nothing in this ecosystem — they're buying access to someone else's audience
What do they want? Revenue, without damaging fan trust or brand equity A business outcome (awareness, acquisition, retention) at a better ROI than alternative marketing channels

If you can't fill in both columns for an organization, you are not ready to build a proposal for them.

7. Step-by-Step Process

How to map the ecosystem for a new prospect or case study, in order:

  1. Identify the Rights Holder. What do they own — venue, audience, media platform, IP? Write a one-sentence description of their asset base.
  2. Identify the audience (Fans). Size, demographics, geography, engagement channels. This is what's actually being sold.
  3. Identify existing Media relationships. Who broadcasts or streams this rights holder's content? Is media rights already sold, or bundled with sponsorship?
  4. Identify current or logical Brand categories. Who is already sponsoring, or who should be, based on audience fit?
  5. Identify Agencies in the picture. Does the brand work through an agency of record? Does the rights holder use a talent or licensing agency?
  6. Identify Venue, Ticketing, and Digital Platform ownership. Are these controlled by the rights holder directly, or by a third party (e.g., a separately owned arena)?
  7. Identify the Commercial Rights Company, if one exists. Is this rights holder already represented by a competitor (Learfield, Legends, IMG, an in-house team)? This changes your entire approach.
  8. Write the one-paragraph ecosystem summary. If you can't summarize all six roles in one paragraph, go back and fill the gaps before moving forward.

8. Real Industry Examples

Notre Dame Athletics / Legends. Legends operates as Notre Dame's commercial partnerships representative across sponsorship, ticketing, and hospitality — a textbook Commercial Rights Company relationship. Notre Dame is the Rights Holder (asset: national brand equity, TV deal with NBC, 80,000-seat stadium); Legends is the outsourced commercial operator; NBC is Media; national and regional brands are the Brand layer.

Formula 1 / Aramco, Heineken, and team title sponsors. F1 is a Rights Holder at the series level, but each of the ten teams is also a Rights Holder in its own right, selling title and technical partnerships independently (e.g., a team's title sponsor). Aramco, as F1's official partner, is a Brand optimizing for global brand association and market entry into markets where motorsport carries prestige. This shows students that "Rights Holder" is not a single fixed layer — it exists at the league level and the team level simultaneously, each with its own commercial rights.

Oak View Group / Climate Pledge Arena. OVG operated as both a venue developer and commercial rights manager, selling the arena's naming rights to Amazon. This is a case where the Commercial Rights Company (OVG) is monetizing an asset — the venue itself — that sits partly outside the sports team that plays there, illustrating why "Venue" is its own stakeholder distinct from the team.

NCAA / ESPN. ESPN's media rights deals with the NCAA and individual conferences show Media functioning as both a buyer of commercial rights (broadcast rights) and a value multiplier for every other sponsorship sold around that inventory — a school's sponsorship packages are worth more specifically because ESPN's cameras are there.

9. VYRO Best Practices

  • Map the ecosystem before you make contact with anyone. A stakeholder map takes 20–30 minutes of research and prevents weeks of misdirected outreach.
  • Identify whether a Commercial Rights Company competitor already represents the prospect. If Learfield, IMG, or an in-house team already holds the mandate, your approach and pitch angle change completely — you are now positioning against an incumbent, not pitching an unrepresented client.
  • Always describe the Fans in specific terms, never "a passionate fanbase." VYRO proposals are built on audience specificity: size, median age, geography, purchase behavior, digital engagement. Vague fan descriptions are the single most common reason internal drafts get sent back by managers.
  • Separate "who owns it" from "who decides." A university athletic director may control sponsorship decisions, but a licensing agency may control merchandise rights, and a conference office may control media rights. Confirm decision rights before building a proposal, not after.
  • Treat the Brand relationship as a business-problem relationship, not a category checklist. "They're a bank, let's pitch them" is not ecosystem mapping — it's guessing. Know what business objective a specific brand is chasing before approaching them (this is developed further in Phase 5: Marketing).

10. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing the Rights Holder with the Commercial Rights Company. A university is the Rights Holder even when VYRO manages its commercial program — VYRO does not own the asset, it manages it on the Rights Holder's behalf.
  • Assuming there are only two parties in the deal. New employees routinely miss agencies, media partners, and venue owners entirely, and are blindsided when one of them turns out to control the actual decision.
  • Describing fans in marketing adjectives instead of data. "Loyal," "passionate," and "engaged" are not audience descriptions — they're filler. A real ecosystem map has numbers.
  • Pitching a brand without knowing if they already have a category competitor sponsoring the same rights holder. Category exclusivity (covered in Phase 2) means a brand may be structurally unable to say yes regardless of fit.
  • Treating VYRO as "the middleman" rather than the outsourced commercial department. This is a positioning mistake that shows up in outreach language — VYRO is not shopping a client's inventory around; VYRO is the client's commercial operation.

11. Professional Tips

  • Keep a running one-page ecosystem map for every active prospect and client — update it every time new information surfaces (a new agency of record, a new media deal, a venue ownership change).
  • When studying a partnership announcement (see Phase 3), the first thing to identify is which stakeholder issued the press release — it tells you who initiated the deal and whose messaging priorities shaped the announcement.
  • Build the habit of asking "who else has to say yes?" on every prospective deal, not just "who do I pitch?"
  • When in doubt about a stakeholder's role, default to asking what they own — ownership almost always reveals the role.

13. Checklists

Ecosystem Map Completion Checklist

  • Rights Holder identified with a one-sentence asset description
  • At least one current or target Brand identified, with their likely business objective named
  • Existing Commercial Rights Company (if any) identified
  • Media/broadcast partner identified
  • Fan audience described with real data, not adjectives
  • Agencies (brand-side and/or rights holder-side) identified or confirmed absent
  • Venue ownership confirmed (same entity as Rights Holder, or separate)
  • Ticketing and digital platform ownership confirmed
  • One-paragraph ecosystem summary written

14. Worksheets

Worksheet: Five Announcements, Five Maps

For five real, published commercial partnership announcements (see Homework), complete a full Six-Party Stakeholder Map for each. For each map, also answer:

  1. Which stakeholder appears to have initiated this deal?
  2. Which stakeholder is absent from the announcement but almost certainly involved (e.g., an agency, a media partner)?
  3. If VYRO had represented the Rights Holder in this deal, what would the pitch to the Brand have emphasized?

15. Homework

  1. Explain the six-party commercial ecosystem from memory, without notes — written, one paragraph per role (six paragraphs total).
  2. Complete five full Six-Party Stakeholder Maps using the template above, based on five real, currently published partnership announcements (industry press, team/league newsroom pages, or brand press releases).
  3. Write a one-page explanation of the difference between sponsorship, advertising, and a commercial partnership — specifically addressing why VYRO's model (outsourced commercial department) is broader than a traditional sponsorship sale.
  4. Draw the complete stakeholder map for the university client scenario described in Business Context (Section 3), even though details are hypothetical — this is a dry run for the Phase 16 Capstone.

Deliverable format: one document containing all four items, submitted for manager review.

16. Manager Coaching Notes

Purpose of this lesson in the employee's development: This is a foundations lesson. Your job as manager is not to test recall — it's to confirm the employee can apply the map to an unfamiliar organization on the spot, out loud, in under two minutes. If they can only recite the definitions, they are not ready for Lesson 1.2.

Discussion questions to ask in review:

  • "Walk me through the ecosystem for [pick an organization not in their homework]." Watch whether they default to two parties (team + brand) or naturally produce all six.
  • "In your five stakeholder maps, which one had the most stakeholders you almost missed? Why did you almost miss them?"
  • "If VYRO were pitching to become the Commercial Rights Company for [a specific prospect], what's the very first thing you'd want to know before writing any outreach?" (Expected answer: whether an incumbent already holds the mandate.)

Expected answers: A proficient employee names all six roles unprompted, can define what each is optimizing for in plain language (not textbook language), and can explain VYRO's position in the ecosystem in one sentence without hedging.

Coaching tips:

  • If an employee's fan descriptions are vague ("passionate fanbase"), send them back to source real numbers before accepting the homework — this is a habit that has to be corrected in Lesson 1, or it recurs through the entire program.
  • If an employee confuses Rights Holder and Commercial Rights Company, use the university example again with a different client type (e.g., a music festival) until the distinction is automatic.
  • Watch for employees who can define the roles but can't identify them in a real announcement — that gap is the actual skill being tested, not definitional recall.

Common employee mistakes to watch for:

  • Treating "Agencies" as optional/skippable in the map.
  • Only two-party thinking under time pressure, even after passing the initial quiz.
  • Describing VYRO as an intermediary/broker rather than the outsourced commercial department.

Evaluation checklist: see Grading Rubric (Section 21).

18. Practical Exercise

Exercise: Live Ecosystem Teardown

Manager assigns one unfamiliar organization (not used in the employee's homework) — a professional sports team, a festival, a conference, or a university not previously discussed. Employee has 30 minutes to research and produce a complete Six-Party Stakeholder Map, unaided by prior notes.

Deliverable: completed template (Section 12) plus a two-minute verbal walkthrough to the manager.

Evaluation focus: speed, completeness, and whether the employee correctly identifies the Rights Holder vs. any Commercial Rights Company already in place.

19. Role-Play Exercise

Scenario: You are a new VYRO account executive. Your manager plays the athletic director of a university that has just parted ways with its previous commercial rights agency. In five minutes, you must ask enough questions to build a first-draft ecosystem map — without pitching anything yet.

Manager's role-play brief (for the person playing the athletic director): Volunteer information only when directly and specifically asked. If the employee asks a vague question ("tell me about your commercial program"), give a vague, unhelpful answer to force a sharper follow-up. Mention an agency and a regional broadcast deal only if asked about media or brand representation — do not offer this information proactively.

What "good" looks like: The employee asks discovery-style questions mapped directly to the six roles (asset ownership, existing brand relationships, existing agency or Commercial Rights Company involvement, media deal status, audience data, venue ownership) rather than jumping straight into a sales pitch.

23. Key Takeaways

  • Every commercial partnership involves at least six roles: Rights Holder, Brand, Commercial Rights Company, Media, Fans, and secondary stakeholders (agencies, venues, ticketing, digital platforms).
  • VYRO is a Commercial Rights Company — the outsourced commercial department for organizations that own valuable assets but lack the internal expertise to monetize them.
  • Fans are the reason any of this has value; describe them with data, never adjectives.
  • Mapping the full ecosystem before making contact prevents the most common and most expensive mistakes new commercial professionals make.
  • "Who else has to say yes?" is a question you will ask on every deal for the rest of your career at VYRO.
