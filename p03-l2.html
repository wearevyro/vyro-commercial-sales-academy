Lesson 3.2 — Analyzing Commercial Rights Companies: VYRO's Peer Set

1. Lesson Overview

Lesson 3.1 taught you to analyze the properties — the leagues, teams, and governing bodies that own commercial rights. This lesson turns the same analytical discipline on the companies that represent those rights on the rights holder's behalf: Learfield, Legends, ASM Global, and Oak View Group.

This is the single most direct competitive research you will do in this academy. These four companies are not case studies in the abstract — they are VYRO's actual peer set. Every one of them competes for the same category of mandate VYRO pursues: becoming an organization's outsourced commercial department. Understanding how each of them operates, what they're genuinely good at, and where their models have structural limitations is what allows a VYRO employee to credibly explain, in a client conversation, why VYRO is the right choice — without resorting to generic claims that could apply to any competitor.

This lesson also introduces a seventh analytical question, specific to competitor case studies, and carries the remaining forty of the fifty total partnership case studies required across Phase 03, along with the "top five analyses" presentation that closes out this phase's homework.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  1. Describe the primary business model and typical client type for each of VYRO's four major peers: Learfield, Legends, ASM Global, and Oak View Group.
  2. Apply the Six-Question Case Study Framework (Lesson 3.1) plus a seventh, competitive-differentiation question to a real deal involving one of these companies.
  3. Correctly distinguish a college multimedia-rights model, a venue-management-embedded sales model, a hospitality/experience-led model, and a venue-development-plus-commercial-rights model.
  4. Articulate, in a client-ready sentence, at least one specific reason a rights holder might choose VYRO over each of the four peer companies, grounded in real structural differences — not generic superiority claims.
  5. Select and present five case studies (from the full fifty completed across Phase 03) that best demonstrate command of the Six-Question Framework and competitive positioning.

3. Business Context

Rights holders evaluating whether to bring on a commercial rights company are almost always comparing multiple options — and in the collegiate and venue space specifically, they are very often comparing VYRO against Learfield, Legends, ASM Global, or Oak View Group by name. A VYRO employee who cannot speak specifically and accurately about how these companies operate will lose credibility the moment a prospective client says, "we're also talking to Learfield — how are you different?"

This is not a lesson in disparaging competitors. It is a lesson in precise, honest structural understanding — because the most persuasive competitive positioning is never "we're better," it's "here's specifically how our model differs, and here's why that difference matters for an organization like yours."

4. Why This Matters

  • Prospective clients will name competitors directly, and often. An employee without a real answer sounds unprepared; an employee with a specific, accurate answer sounds like a trusted advisor.
  • Each peer company's model has genuine strengths and genuine structural limitations — understanding both, honestly, is what allows VYRO to position itself accurately rather than through generic claims that a savvy client will see through immediately.
  • This research directly informs Phase 12 (Business Development), where competitive positioning becomes a core skill for winning new client mandates.

5. Core Lesson

5.1 Why Study Competitors With the Same Rigor as Properties

The Six-Question Framework from Lesson 3.1 works on any commercial deal — but analyzing a competitor's deal requires one additional layer: not just understanding what happened, but understanding what it reveals about how that competitor operates, and what VYRO would realistically do differently in the same situation.

5.2 Four Peer Models

Learfield — best known for college multimedia and marketing rights agreements with university athletic departments, frequently structured around a guaranteed rights fee (sometimes with revenue-share upside) paid to the university in exchange for the exclusive right to manage and sell the school's multimedia and sponsorship rights. This model transfers meaningful financial risk onto the rights company itself, which is a structural choice with real tradeoffs: it can win mandates on financial certainty, but it also means the rights company must be highly confident in its own sales capability before signing.

Legends — operates across sponsorship sales, hospitality, and ticketing/experience services, frequently for marquee properties (major venues, high-profile athletic departments). Legends' model leans heavily into the experience and hospitality side of commercial rights — premium seating, VIP programs — alongside traditional sponsorship sales, reflecting a business built partly out of a stadium/venue hospitality heritage.

ASM Global — primarily a venue management company (operating arenas, stadiums, and convention centers on behalf of their owners) with commercial rights and sponsorship sales functions embedded within that broader venue operations mandate. ASM Global's commercial rights work is typically one part of a much larger venue-operations relationship, not a standalone service — an important structural distinction from a pure-play commercial rights company.

Oak View Group (OVG) — combines venue development (building or renovating arenas and stadiums) with commercial rights management, frequently securing large naming-rights deals as part of new venue projects it develops or manages. OVG's model illustrates a company that captures commercial rights value partly through its role in the physical creation of the asset itself, not only through representing an asset that already exists.

5.3 The Seventh Question: Competitive Differentiation

For any competitor case study, add this question to the standard six from Lesson 3.1:

7. If VYRO had this mandate instead, what would VYRO do differently — and why would that difference matter to this specific rights holder?

This question requires you to have a real, specific answer — not "VYRO would do a better job." A strong answer names a structural difference (scope of service, risk model, relationship depth, category focus) and ties it to something the specific rights holder in the case study would plausibly care about.

7. Step-by-Step Process

How to analyze a competitor case study:

  1. Identify which of the four peer companies is involved and confirm which core model (Section 5.2) applies.
  2. Apply the Six-Question Framework from Lesson 3.1 in full.
  3. Add the Seventh Question: what would VYRO do differently, and why would that matter to this specific rights holder?
  4. Cross-reference against the Peer Company Comparison Matrix to confirm your answer is grounded in a real structural difference, not a generic claim.
  5. Write the final answer as a client-ready sentence — something you could actually say out loud in a competitive pitch conversation.

8. Real Industry Examples

A university athletic department's multimedia rights agreement with Learfield — illustrates the guaranteed-fee model in action, and is a useful case for practicing the Seventh Question: what would a VYRO engagement look like for the same university under a different risk/compensation structure, and what would that change about the relationship?

Legends' role in premium hospitality and sponsorship sales for a marquee venue or athletic department (e.g., Notre Dame, referenced in Phase 01) — illustrates the experience-led model, and is a useful case for practicing how VYRO would balance hospitality-led value against a broader full-lifecycle commercial rights mandate.

ASM Global's operation of a major arena or convention center, with commercial rights as one component of a broader venue management contract — illustrates a case where "who represents the commercial rights" is not a standalone question but part of a much larger venue-operations decision, which changes the competitive conversation entirely.

Oak View Group's involvement in a new arena development and its associated naming rights deal (e.g., Climate Pledge Arena, referenced in Phase 01) — illustrates how commercial rights value can be captured through involvement in the physical creation of a venue, a model VYRO generally does not replicate, making this a clear, honest point of structural differentiation to articulate.

9. VYRO Best Practices

  • Always name the specific structural difference, not a vague superiority claim, when discussing how VYRO compares to a named competitor in front of a client.
  • Acknowledge genuine competitor strengths honestly — a rights holder will trust VYRO's competitive positioning more, not less, if it doesn't pretend competitors have no real strengths.
  • Match the competitive argument to what the specific rights holder actually cares about — a university weighing Learfield's guaranteed-fee certainty against VYRO's model needs a different conversation than a venue owner weighing ASM Global's bundled operations model.
  • Keep the Peer Company Comparison Matrix current — these companies' models and typical deal structures evolve, and stale competitive knowledge is worse than no competitive knowledge, because it can lead to inaccurate claims in a client conversation.

10. Common Mistakes

  • Making generic "we're better" claims without a structural basis — this is immediately recognizable to any client who has actually spoken with multiple commercial rights companies, and it damages credibility.
  • Confusing ASM Global's venue-management-embedded model with a pure-play commercial rights company model — this is a common and important distinction to get right, since it changes the entire nature of the competitive conversation.
  • Speaking negatively or dismissively about a named competitor — professional, structural comparison lands far better than disparagement, and reflects VYRO's standard for how employees represent the company.
  • Skipping the Seventh Question entirely and treating a competitor case study identically to a Lesson 3.1 property case study — the competitive-differentiation layer is the entire point of this lesson.

11. Professional Tips

  • Build your Seventh Question answers as reusable talking points — over time, this becomes a real internal competitive-positioning reference for the whole team, not just a homework exercise.
  • When researching a peer company's deal, look specifically for what services were and weren't included — service scope is often the clearest, most factual point of differentiation available, more reliable than inferring quality or client satisfaction from public information alone.
  • Practice delivering Seventh Question answers verbally, out loud, the same way you'd practice a sales pitch — competitive positioning is a spoken skill as much as a written one.

16. Manager Coaching Notes

Purpose of this lesson in the employee's development: This lesson tests whether the employee can hold two things at once: genuine, honest understanding of a competitor's model, and confident, specific positioning of VYRO against it. Managers should watch for employees who swing too far in either direction — either speaking generically about competitors (a knowledge gap) or speaking dismissively about them (a professionalism gap).

Discussion questions to ask in review:

  • "A university tells you they're also talking to Learfield. What do you say?" (Tests real-time application of the Seventh Question, live.)
  • "What's the actual structural difference between ASM Global and a pure-play commercial rights company like VYRO?"
  • "Walk me through your top five case study selections — why these five specifically?"

Expected answers: A proficient employee names specific, accurate structural differences for each competitor without resorting to generic claims or disparagement, and can defend their top-five selection with clear reasoning tied to the Six/Seven-Question Framework.

Coaching tips:

  • If an employee's competitive answers are vague, role-play the "we're also talking to [competitor]" scenario repeatedly until answers are specific and confident.
  • If an employee's top-five selection is weak, use it as a teaching moment about self-evaluation — the ability to recognize your own strongest work is itself a professional skill being assessed here.

Common employee mistakes to watch for:

  • Generic competitive claims without structural grounding.
  • Confusing ASM Global's venue-management model with a pure-play commercial rights model.
  • Selecting top-five case studies based on which property is most famous rather than which analysis is strongest.

Evaluation checklist: see Grading Rubric (Section 20).

18. Practical Exercise

Exercise: Live Competitive Response

Manager names one of the four peer companies and gives a brief, realistic client scenario ("We're also getting a proposal from [Competitor] — why should we go with VYRO instead?"). Employee has 2 minutes to prepare and then delivers a verbal response.

Evaluation focus: specificity, honesty about competitor strengths, and whether the response is grounded in real structural difference rather than generic claims.

19. Role-Play Exercise

Scenario: You are in a final-round pitch meeting. Your manager plays a rights holder decision-maker who has already met with two of VYRO's competitors and is visibly comparing VYRO against both.

Manager's role-play brief: Name two specific competitors (chosen from the four covered in this lesson) and ask the employee to explain, for each, why VYRO might be a better fit — without being told which two in advance, forcing real-time recall.

What "good" looks like: The employee responds to each competitor separately and specifically, correctly identifies which structural differences are actually relevant to this particular rights holder's situation, and never resorts to generic or dismissive language.
