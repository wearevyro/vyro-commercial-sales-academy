Lesson 9.1 — Activation Planning & Execution Channels

1. Lesson Overview

A signed contract is only the beginning. Everything through Phase 08 built and sold a package; this phase brings it to life. As introduced in Phase 02, Activation is the ongoing execution of a partnership beyond its signed rights — and a right that's sold but never activated delivers little real value to either side. A brand that signs a strong package but experiences weak activation will not renew, no matter how well the original deal was priced and packaged.

This lesson covers activation planning as a discipline, plus eight specific execution channels: social campaigns, sampling programs, hospitality, VIP experiences, community outreach, influencer partnerships, digital campaigns, and content creation. Lesson 9.2 covers measurement and reporting — proving activation actually worked — and assembles everything into this phase's core homework deliverable: a full-year activation calendar for one sponsor.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  1. Explain why activation planning must begin before a deal closes, not after, and connect it directly to Phase 04's first-30-days relationship plan.
  2. Define and give a concrete example of each of the eight execution channels covered in this lesson.
  3. Map specific activation channels to the specific commercial assets (Phase 06) and tier-level activation ideas (Phase 08) they bring to life.
  4. Build a structured activation plan sequenced across a full partnership calendar (pre-season, in-season, off-season/renewal window).
  5. Avoid the most common activation execution failures that damage otherwise strong partnerships.

3. Business Context

VYRO's long-term relationship model (Phase 01) depends entirely on activation actually happening well. A rights holder can sell the same package twice — once at signature, and again, effectively, through strong activation that earns renewal. Activation is also where VYRO's account management team (Phase 10 develops this further) spends the majority of its ongoing time with an existing client, making this phase's skills central to the day-to-day work of managing a real, live partnership.

4. Why This Matters

  • Unactivated rights are invisible value. A signage asset nobody promotes, a hospitality suite nobody fills, a digital right nobody uses — all represent real, priced value going unrealized.
  • Weak activation is the most common reason strong deals don't renew — not poor initial packaging or pricing, but a partnership that simply didn't feel alive to the brand during the term.
  • Activation planning done in advance (not improvised after signature) is what allows VYRO to deliver consistently, rather than scrambling reactively once a partnership is already underway.

5. Core Lesson

5.1 Activation Planning

Activation planning is the discipline of translating a signed package into a specific, scheduled, resourced plan of execution — ideally drafted before the deal closes (directly extending Phase 04's first-30-days relationship plan requirement). A strong activation plan answers, for every included asset: what specifically will be done to bring it to life, when, and who is responsible for executing it. Activation planning is not a one-time document — it's a living plan reviewed and adjusted throughout the partnership term.

5.2 Social Campaigns

Coordinated, planned social content specifically built around the partnership — co-branded posts, campaign hashtags, contest or promotion mechanics — distinct from simply "mentioning" the sponsor occasionally. Social campaigns should be planned with a specific cadence and specific content themes tied to the broader partnership calendar, not produced ad hoc.

5.3 Sampling Programs

Product sampling activations — giving fans direct, physical access to a brand's product at games or events. Particularly relevant for Supplier-tier partnerships (Phase 02) where in-kind product value is part of the deal structure, but valuable across tiers for any brand with a physical, sampleable product.

5.4 Hospitality and VIP Experiences

The actual delivery of the hospitality and access-based assets catalogued in Phase 06 and included in the package sold in Phase 08 — suites filled, VIP experiences scheduled and delivered, meet-and-greets actually executed. This is where the Countable Unit Conversion from Phase 06 (Lesson 6.2) becomes real: a package that promised "6 suite tickets per home game" needs an actual operational process ensuring those seats are used, not left empty.

5.5 Community Outreach

Activating community program-based assets (Phase 06) — brand involvement in clinics, camps, or outreach events — particularly valuable for CSR and Employer Branding-oriented partnerships (Phase 05). Community outreach activation should be planned with specific, real events and dates, not left as a vague, unscheduled commitment.

5.6 Influencer Partnerships

Executing the influencer/athlete component of a blended-channel package (Phase 05) — coordinating content, appearances, or endorsement-adjacent activity with individual creators or athletes affiliated with the property. This must respect the boundary established in Phase 06 between rights holder-controlled inventory and an individual athlete's personal NIL rights — influencer activation involving a specific athlete requires that athlete's own separate agreement, not just the rights holder's sign-off.

5.7 Digital Campaigns and Content Creation

Executing digital rights and Brand Integration assets (Phase 02, Phase 06) — website/app placements actually built and live, co-branded content actually produced and published. Content Creation activation should be planned with a specific production calendar and specific deliverables (e.g., "three athlete profile videos, published monthly across Q2"), not an open-ended commitment to "create some content."

Framework: The Activation Channel Menu

Maps each execution channel back to the asset categories it activates:

SOCIAL CAMPAIGNS          → Digital & Owned Channel assets (Phase 06)
SAMPLING PROGRAMS         → Supplier-tier / in-kind product value (Phase 02)
HOSPITALITY / VIP         → Access & Experience assets (Phase 06)

INFLUENCER PARTNERSHIPS   → Blended-channel influencer/athlete component (Phase 05)

Use this menu to confirm every major asset category sold in a package (Phase 08) has a corresponding, planned activation channel — an asset with no mapped activation channel is a gap in the plan.

PRE-SEASON / ONBOARDING (first 30 days, extending Phase 04)
     ↓
IN-SEASON (regular cadence: social, hospitality, sampling, content — tied to the property's actual event calendar)
     ↓

     ↓
OFF-SEASON / RENEWAL WINDOW (community outreach often concentrates here; measurement and reporting intensify — see Lesson 9.2)

7. Step-by-Step Process

How to build a channel-by-channel activation plan for a signed package:

  1. Review the full list of assets included in the sold package (Phase 08's Final Package Ladder Document for the relevant tier).
  2. Apply the Activation Channel Menu to confirm every major asset category has a mapped activation channel.
  3. For each channel, specify concrete deliverables, not vague commitments — using the Countable Unit Conversion discipline from Phase 06.
  4. Sequence all planned activation across the Activation Calendar Skeleton, aligned to the property's real event/season calendar.
  5. Confirm any influencer/athlete-involving activation has the necessary separate athlete-level agreement in place before scheduling.
  6. Assign clear responsibility (VYRO, the rights holder, or the brand) for executing each planned activation item.

8. Real Industry Examples

A sponsor's suite consistently filled with the sponsor's own clients across a full season, coordinated through a proactive scheduling process rather than left to the sponsor to remember and organize independently — illustrates Section 5.4's operational delivery discipline in practice.

A co-branded social content series planned with a specific cadence and themes tied to the property's actual competitive calendar (e.g., content around key games or events rather than randomly timed posts) — illustrates Section 5.2's planned cadence principle.

A Supplier-tier sponsor executing a structured sampling program at a defined set of home games across a season, rather than an unscheduled, occasional presence — illustrates Section 5.3's need for specific, calendared execution.

A community clinic series scheduled specifically during a property's off-season, when athletes and facilities have more available capacity — illustrates the Activation Calendar Skeleton's guidance that certain activation types naturally concentrate in specific windows.

9. VYRO Best Practices

  • Begin activation planning before the deal closes, extending Phase 04's first-30-days relationship plan directly into a full-term activation calendar.
  • Map every major sold asset to a specific activation channel using the Activation Channel Menu — an unmapped asset is a planning gap, not a minor oversight.
  • Specify concrete, countable activation deliverables, never vague commitments — this is the same discipline from Phase 06 applied to execution rather than inventory.
  • Confirm athlete-level agreements separately before scheduling any influencer/athlete activation — this protects both the rights holder and VYRO from overcommitting inventory that isn't fully secured.
  • Treat the activation plan as living, not static — review and adjust it at real checkpoints throughout the partnership term, not just once at the start.

10. Common Mistakes

  • Waiting until after signature to begin activation planning, producing a slow, disorganized start to the relationship.
  • Leaving major sold assets without any mapped activation channel, meaning real, paid-for value goes undelivered.
  • Producing vague activation commitments ("some social content," "occasional hospitality") instead of specific, scheduled deliverables.
  • Scheduling influencer/athlete activation without confirming the separate athlete-level agreement is actually in place.
  • Treating the activation plan as a one-time document rather than a living plan reviewed throughout the term.

11. Professional Tips

  • Build activation plans using the same Countable Unit discipline learned in Phase 06 — a plan full of specific, scheduled deliverables is both easier to execute and easier to report on later (Lesson 9.2).
  • Align activation timing to the property's real, existing event calendar whenever possible — activation that piggybacks on a property's natural high-attention moments performs better than activation scheduled independently of them.
  • Keep a running personal library of strong activation ideas by channel, drawn from your Phase 08 Activation Idea Bank work and ongoing industry observation — this becomes genuinely useful reference material for every future client engagement.

16. Manager Coaching Notes

Purpose of this lesson in the employee's development: This lesson tests operational planning discipline — whether an employee can turn a sold package into a genuinely executable plan, not just a list of nice ideas. Managers should focus review specifically on specificity and completeness of channel mapping.

Discussion questions to ask in review:

  • "Walk me through one asset from your package that didn't have an obvious activation channel — how did you handle it?"
  • "Pick one activation deliverable from your plan — is it specific and countable enough that someone else could execute it without asking you clarifying questions?"
  • "Where did you place your community outreach activation on the calendar, and why there specifically?"

Expected answers: A proficient employee can point to how they resolved a genuinely tricky asset-to-channel mapping, can defend the specificity of a chosen deliverable, and has a deliberate (not arbitrary) rationale for calendar placement.

Coaching tips:

  • If any major asset lacks a mapped activation channel, send the plan back — this is a core completeness requirement, not a minor gap.
  • If deliverables remain vague despite the worksheet, have the employee rewrite three deliverables live, in the room, applying the Countable Unit discipline directly.

Common employee mistakes to watch for:

  • Unmapped assets.
  • Vague, uncountable activation deliverables.
  • Influencer/athlete activation scheduled without confirming separate athlete agreement status.

Evaluation checklist: see Grading Rubric (Section 20).

18. Practical Exercise

Exercise: Rapid Channel Mapping

Manager provides a summary of an unfamiliar sold package (5–7 assets). Employee has 20 minutes to map every asset to a specific activation channel and draft one concrete deliverable for each.

Evaluation focus: completeness of mapping (no unmapped assets) and specificity of deliverables.

19. Role-Play Exercise

Scenario: You're presenting your draft activation plan to the rights holder client for their input and sign-off. Your manager plays this client, who is concerned the plan is too ambitious to actually execute with their limited internal staff capacity.

Manager's role-play brief: Raise a specific concern: "we don't have staff to coordinate weekly social content and manage hospitality every home game — this feels like too much for us." Push for a concrete answer, not just reassurance.

What "good" looks like: The employee responds with a specific, realistic adjustment — clarifying which parts of the plan VYRO will own directly versus what requires rights holder involvement, and where the plan can be resourced or simplified — rather than either dismissing the concern or abandoning the plan's ambition entirely.

23. Key Takeaways

  • Activation is the ongoing execution that turns a signed right into real, delivered value — a right sold but never activated delivers little real value to either side.
  • Activation planning should begin before a deal closes, directly extending Phase 04's first-30-days relationship plan.
  • Eight core execution channels — social campaigns, sampling, hospitality/VIP, community outreach, influencer partnerships, digital campaigns, and content creation — map back to specific asset categories from Phase 06.
  • Every activation deliverable should be specific and countable, never vague, using the same discipline learned in Phase 06's inventory work.
  • Influencer/athlete activation always requires a separate, confirmed athlete-level agreement — never assume rights holder sign-off alone is sufficient.
