Lesson 8.2 — Pricing, Building & Presenting the Five Packages

1. Lesson Overview

Lesson 8.1 taught you to design a coherent five-tier ladder. This lesson teaches you to finish the job: price each tier correctly, attach real activation ideas, write the business-objective framing that makes each tier sell itself, and assemble the whole thing into a professional proposal document you could actually hand to a client or present to a brand.

This lesson produces this phase's core homework deliverable and one of the most important artifacts in this entire academy: a complete Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum, and Founding Partner package set, each with real pricing, real assets, real activation ideas, and a real business objective — built entirely on the design principles from Lesson 8.1 and the skills from every phase before it.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  1. Price a bundled package correctly, applying bundle discount logic while protecting the exclusivity premium at top tiers.
  2. Attach specific, concrete activation ideas to each tier, not generic placeholders.
  3. Write a clear, specific business-objective statement for each tier that communicates who it's built for and why.
  4. Assemble a complete, professional package proposal document covering all five tiers.
  5. Present a full package ladder to a prospective brand in a structured, persuasive way.

3. Business Context

A priced, well-designed package ladder is the tangible product a VYRO account executive actually presents in a pitch. Everything before this lesson — terminology, research, assets, valuation, design — exists to produce this one deliverable. A rights holder judges VYRO's value substantially on the strength of exactly this kind of document; a brand's decision to engage seriously (or not) often depends on whether the package in front of them reads as a thoughtfully built solution or a generic price list.

4. Why This Matters

  • Bundle pricing done carelessly either overprices or underprices a package relative to its individual components — get this wrong and either lose the deal on price or leave real revenue on the table.
  • Generic activation ideas make a package feel abstract — specific, concrete activation ideas (Phase 09 previews this fully) make a brand able to visualize the partnership actually happening.
  • A poorly assembled proposal document undermines even excellent underlying work — the final presentation is what a client and a brand actually see and judge VYRO by.

5. Core Lesson

5.1 Bundle Pricing Logic

A package price is not simply the sum of its individually valued assets (Phase 07). Two forces pull in opposite directions: bundle discount logic (a package typically prices modestly below the straight sum of its individual asset values, because bundling itself creates convenience value for the buyer and incentivizes a larger overall commitment) and exclusivity premium protection (at the top tiers, genuine category exclusivity can offset or exceed the bundle discount, since exclusivity's value doesn't simply add — it multiplies the value of everything else in the package by removing competitive dilution). The correct approach: sum the individual Phase 07 valuations for all included assets, apply a modest bundle discount (typically reflecting genuine buyer convenience, not a giveaway), then add back the exclusivity premium as its own distinct, quantified line where applicable — consistent with Lesson 7.2's requirement to never fold exclusivity invisibly into a base number.

5.2 Activation Ideas Per Tier

Every tier needs at least one specific, concrete activation idea — not a placeholder like "social media activation," but something like "a season-long co-branded content series featuring three athlete profile videos, distributed across the property's owned channels and the sponsor's own social presence." Activation ideas should scale with tier: entry tiers get simpler, lower-cost activation; top tiers get deeper, more resource-intensive activation matching their investment level. This directly previews Phase 09's full activation planning skill set.

5.3 Business Objective Statement Per Tier

Using Lesson 8.1's Objective-to-Tier Mapping, write an explicit, one-to-two sentence statement for each tier describing who it's built for and what problem it solves — this is real proposal copy, not an internal note. Example structure: "[Tier name] is built for brands pursuing [objective], offering [key differentiating asset] to deliver [specific business outcome]."

5.4 Building the Proposal Document

Assemble all five tiers into one coherent document with a consistent structure for every tier: tier name, business objective statement, included assets (organized clearly, using Phase 02 terminology), activation ideas, and price. The document should read as one coherent product line, not five disconnected proposals stapled together — a brand reading the full document should be able to see the ladder's logic at a glance.

5.5 Presenting the Ladder

When presenting a package ladder live to a brand, generally present strategically rather than starting from the cheapest option — often anchoring with the top tier's full value proposition first (even if the brand is unlikely to buy Founding Partner) establishes the ladder's overall value ceiling and makes the tier they ultimately choose feel like good value by comparison. Always connect the recommended tier explicitly back to the brand's specific business objective (Phase 05), rather than presenting the ladder as a generic menu.

6. Commercial Frameworks

Framework: Bundle Pricing Formula

SUM of individual Phase 07 asset valuations (anchor prices) for all included assets
        −
BUNDLE DISCOUNT (modest — reflects buyer convenience, not a giveaway)
        +
EXCLUSIVITY PREMIUM (quantified separately, where applicable — can offset or exceed the discount)
        =
FINAL TIER PRICE

Framework: Standard Package Proposal Structure

TIER NAME
  Business Objective Statement (who this is for, what it solves)
  Included Assets (organized by category, using Phase 02 terminology)
  Activation Ideas (specific, concrete, scaled to tier)
  Price

Repeat consistently for all five tiers within one unified document.

7. Step-by-Step Process

How to finalize and present a full package ladder:

  1. Start from Lesson 8.1's Five-Tier Ladder Sketch, with assets allocated per tier.
  2. For each tier, sum the individual Phase 07 valuations of included assets.
  3. Apply a modest, justified bundle discount to each tier's summed value.
  4. Add back the exclusivity premium as a distinct line where genuine exclusivity is included (primarily top tiers).
  5. Write the business objective statement for each tier, using Lesson 8.1's Objective-to-Tier Mapping.
  6. Attach at least one specific, concrete activation idea per tier, scaled appropriately to tier level.
  7. Assemble all five tiers into the Standard Package Proposal Structure as one coherent document.
  8. Practice presenting the ladder, anchoring strategically and connecting the recommended tier to a specific business objective.

8. Real Industry Examples

A Founding Partner package presented first in a pitch meeting to establish the ladder's overall value ceiling, even when the brand's realistic budget points toward a Gold-level commitment — illustrates Section 5.5's anchoring strategy in practice.

A Platinum tier priced with a modest bundle discount on its base assets, but with a distinct, separately stated exclusivity premium that makes its final price notably higher than a simple sum-minus-discount calculation would suggest — illustrates Section 5.1's dual-force pricing logic directly.

A Bronze/Supplier tier with a simple, low-cost activation idea (e.g., a single co-branded social post series) contrasted with a Founding Partner tier's much deeper activation commitment (a season-long content series, dedicated hospitality events, and community program integration) — illustrates activation scaling appropriately across the ladder.

9. VYRO Best Practices

  • Never price a package as a simple, unadjusted sum of individual asset values — always apply bundle discount and exclusivity premium logic deliberately.
  • Attach a genuinely specific activation idea to every tier, never a generic placeholder — this is one of the clearest signals of proposal quality to a sophisticated brand.
  • Write business objective statements as real, usable proposal copy, not internal shorthand — they should be ready to present as-is.
  • Present the full ladder as one coherent system in both the document and the live pitch, never as disconnected individual options.
  • Anchor strategically when presenting live, and always connect the recommended tier back to the specific brand's stated objective from discovery (Phase 04).

10. Common Mistakes

  • Pricing a package as a raw sum of individual asset values, ignoring both bundle discount and exclusivity premium logic.
  • Using generic, non-specific activation ideas ("social media promotion," "digital activation") instead of concrete, scaled ideas.
  • Writing business objective statements as internal notes rather than client-ready proposal copy.
  • Presenting the ladder as five disconnected options rather than one coherent, strategically ordered system.
  • Failing to connect the recommended tier back to the specific brand's actual objective, presenting the ladder as a generic menu instead.

11. Professional Tips

  • Calculate the raw sum of individual asset values first, then apply the discount and exclusivity premium as two clearly separated, visible steps — never collapse the math into a single unexplained final number, even in your own working notes.
  • Draft two or three activation idea candidates per tier before selecting one — the discipline of comparing real alternatives produces stronger final choices than writing down the first idea that comes to mind.
  • Rehearse the live ladder presentation out loud at least once before delivering it for real — anchoring and objective-specific framing both land better with practiced, natural delivery than a first read-through.

14. Worksheets

Worksheet: Activation Idea Bank

For each of the five tiers in your ladder, brainstorm and select the single strongest activation idea from at least three candidate ideas per tier — document all candidates considered and your reasoning for the final selection.

15. Homework

  1. Complete the Activation Idea Bank worksheet for all five tiers.
  2. Finalize the complete Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum, and Founding Partner packages for your Phase 08 organization (building directly on Lesson 8.1's ladder sketch), each including: correctly calculated pricing (Bundle Pricing Formula), a complete list of included assets, at least one specific activation idea, and a client-ready business objective statement.
  3. Assemble the full Final Package Ladder Document, formatted and ready to present.
  4. Prepare a five-minute live presentation of the full ladder, using strategic anchoring, for delivery in the practical exercise/certification.

Deliverable format: one complete, professional Final Package Ladder Document, plus the Activation Idea Bank worksheet.

16. Manager Coaching Notes

Purpose of this lesson in the employee's development: This lesson tests whether an employee can produce a truly client-ready deliverable — the actual document VYRO would present in real business development work. Managers should evaluate this homework with the same standard they'd apply to a real client proposal, not a training exercise.

Discussion questions to ask in review:

  • "Walk me through your Platinum tier's pricing — show me the math: sum, discount, exclusivity premium."
  • "Pick your favorite activation idea from the Activation Idea Bank — why did it beat the other candidates?"
  • "If I'm a brand and you're presenting this ladder to me, where do you start, and why?"

Expected answers: A proficient employee can show transparent, correctly reasoned pricing math for any tier on request, can defend their activation idea selection against real alternatives they considered, and has a clear, deliberate presentation strategy rather than a default bottom-up walkthrough.

Coaching tips:

  • If pricing shows a raw sum with no discount or exclusivity logic applied, send it back — this is a core skill from this lesson, not an optional refinement.
  • If activation ideas remain generic despite the worksheet, have the employee generate three genuinely different candidate ideas for one tier live, in the room, to demonstrate the skill directly.

Common employee mistakes to watch for:

  • Raw-sum pricing with no bundle/exclusivity logic.
  • Generic activation ideas.
  • Presentation strategy defaulting to a flat bottom-up walkthrough with no anchoring.

Evaluation checklist: see Grading Rubric (Section 21).

18. Practical Exercise

Exercise: Live Ladder Presentation

Employee delivers the prepared five-minute presentation of their full package ladder to the manager, who plays a prospective brand contact with a stated objective (assigned by the manager in advance, drawn from Phase 05's nine objectives).

Evaluation focus: whether the employee's presentation strategy, tier emphasis, and framing correctly adapt to the assigned objective, and whether pricing and activation details are presented confidently and accurately when questioned.

19. Role-Play Exercise

Scenario: Mid-presentation, the brand contact (played by your manager) asks a direct, specific pricing question: "walk me through exactly how you got to this Platinum number."

Manager's role-play brief: Interrupt the presentation naturally at a reasonable point to ask this question, and follow up with "so what's the actual discount you're applying, and why that amount?" if the initial answer is vague.

What "good" looks like: The employee can immediately and transparently walk through the Bundle Pricing Formula for the specific tier in question — sum, discount, exclusivity premium — without losing composure or resorting to vague reassurance.

23. Key Takeaways

  • Package pricing combines a modest bundle discount with a separately quantified exclusivity premium — never a raw, unadjusted sum.
  • Every tier needs a specific, concrete activation idea scaled to its investment level — generic placeholders undermine the proposal's credibility.
  • Business objective statements should be written as real, client-ready proposal copy, connecting each tier to a specific buyer need from Phase 05.
  • The five tiers should be assembled and presented as one coherent system, strategically anchored, not five disconnected options.
  • This lesson's Final Package Ladder Document is a genuinely usable artifact — the direct, tangible product of everything taught across Phases 02 through 08.
