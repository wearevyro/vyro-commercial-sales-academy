Lesson 6.1 — Physical & Rights-Based Assets

1. Lesson Overview

Phase 02 taught you the terminology for commercial rights. Phase 05 taught you what brands want. This phase teaches you the skill that connects them: building a complete, professional asset inventory — the exhaustive, catalogued list of everything a rights holder owns that could be commercialized.

Asset inventory development is one of VYRO's first and most foundational deliverables in any new client engagement (introduced in Phase 02, Lesson 2.1). A rights holder that has never been through a proper commercial audit almost always underestimates what it actually owns — most organizations can name their obvious assets (jersey sponsorship, stadium signage) but miss dozens of smaller, real, sellable assets hiding in plain sight.

This lesson covers the physical and rights-based asset categories: venue naming rights (stadium, arena, training facility), jersey sponsorship, venue signage, broadcast and streaming rights, licensing, and merchandise. Lesson 6.2 covers digital, experiential, and community-based assets. Together, these two lessons build the skill tested directly in this phase's homework: cataloguing at least 150 real commercial assets for a single organization.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  1. Define and give examples of each physical/rights-based asset category: naming rights (three sub-types), jersey sponsorship, venue signage, broadcast rights, streaming rights, licensing, and merchandise.
  2. Apply the Asset Audit Walkthrough method to systematically identify physical assets across every zone of a venue or organization.
  3. Identify the "rights layer stack" for a given physical asset — which specific commercial rights (naming, media, digital, licensing) attach to it simultaneously.
  4. Distinguish assets that are currently sold, currently unsold, and not yet identified at all within a rights holder's existing program.
  5. Build a substantial, real, well-organized physical/rights-based asset inventory for a real organization.

3. Business Context

When VYRO takes on a new rights holder client, one of the very first deliverables is a Commercial Rights Audit — a systematic inventory of everything the client owns that could generate revenue. This is not a one-time formality; it is the foundation every later phase depends on. Valuation (Phase 07) cannot happen without a complete inventory to value. Packaging (Phase 08) cannot happen without inventory to package. A rights holder that believes it has "some sponsorship opportunities" and a rights holder that has been through a real VYRO audit and knows it has, for example, 180 distinct catalogued assets across a dozen categories are in fundamentally different negotiating and revenue positions.

4. Why This Matters

  • Undiscovered inventory is unsold revenue. Every asset missed in an audit is potential revenue VYRO's client will never realize.
  • A thin, generic inventory produces thin, generic packages. Phase 08's packaging work is only as strong as the inventory feeding it.
  • Systematic auditing prevents the most common inventory mistake: only cataloguing the "obvious" assets (jersey, main stadium signage) while missing dozens of smaller, real, sellable assets that a less rigorous process would never surface.

5. Core Lesson

5.1 Naming Rights — Three Sub-Types

Stadium/Arena Naming Rights — the right to attach a brand name to the primary competition venue itself. Typically the single largest individual revenue line in an entire commercial program (introduced in Phase 02).

Training Facility Naming Rights — the right to attach a brand name to a practice or training facility, distinct from the primary competition venue. Often undervalued or entirely overlooked by rights holders auditing their own inventory, despite being a real, sellable, distinct asset — particularly valuable to brands targeting a more insider/dedicated-fan or recruiting-adjacent audience than general venue naming rights.

Program/Component Naming Rights — naming rights scoped to a specific program, entrance, or venue component rather than the whole facility (e.g., a specific gate, a specific practice court, a specific team room) — smaller in scale than full venue naming rights, but real, distinct, sellable inventory that a thorough audit should always identify.

5.2 Jersey Sponsorship

The right to place a brand's mark on team uniforms, typically one of the most visible and most consistently in-demand assets a team-based rights holder can offer, subject to any league or governing body rules on placement, size, or category.

5.3 Venue Signage

Physical or digital branding placed within a venue — scoreboard signage, concourse signage, seating-bowl signage, exterior signage, digital ribbon boards. A thorough audit catalogues signage location by location, not as one generic "signage" line item — each physical location is a distinct, separately sellable asset with its own visibility profile and value.

5.4 Broadcast Rights and Streaming Rights

Broadcast Rights — the right to televise live or recorded coverage of events, distinct from any sponsorship sold around that coverage (Phase 02). Streaming Rights — the right to distribute coverage via digital/streaming platforms, increasingly sold separately from traditional broadcast rights as audiences fragment across platforms. A rights holder may hold these rights directly, have them held by a conference/league (Phase 03's federated archetype), or have them split across multiple parties — auditing which is the case is a required first step, not an assumption.

5.5 Licensing and Merchandise

Licensing — the right to produce goods using the rights holder's IP (Phase 02). Merchandise — the physical products themselves, sold either directly by the rights holder or through licensed third parties. These are related but distinct audit line items: a rights holder may have strong merchandise sales through a licensee they don't directly control, meaning the licensing relationship itself — not the merchandise revenue — is the actual commercial asset available for a new brand partnership (e.g., a co-branded product line requiring separate licensing negotiation).

5.6 The Rights Layer Stack

Almost every physical asset carries more than one type of commercial right simultaneously. A stadium naming rights deal typically also carries digital rights (the name appears across the rights holder's digital channels), media rights exposure (the name is heard in every broadcast mention), and sometimes licensing implications (the name may appear on official merchandise). A thorough audit identifies the full rights layer stack for each physical asset, not just its most obvious single right — this materially affects both valuation (Phase 07) and packaging (Phase 08).

6. Commercial Frameworks

Framework: The Asset Audit Walkthrough

Systematically move through every physical zone of a venue or organization, cataloguing assets zone by zone:

EXTERIOR (signage, entrance naming, parking/lot naming)
     ↓
CONCOURSE (signage, kiosks, digital displays, sampling zones)
     ↓
SEATING BOWL (scoreboard, ribbon boards, seat-back/aisle signage, premium sections)
     ↓
PLAYING SURFACE / COURT / FIELD (field-level signage, on-field/court branding where permitted)
     ↓
TRAINING & LOCKER FACILITIES (naming rights, equipment/apparel branding)
     ↓
BROADCAST & DIGITAL (broadcast mentions, streaming platform integration, social/digital channels)
     ↓
MERCHANDISE & LICENSING (licensed product lines, official retail partnerships)

Never skip a zone, even if it seems unlikely to yield real inventory — the most common audit failure is stopping early because the "obvious" assets have already been found.

Framework: The Rights Layer Stack (see 5.6)

For every physical asset identified, ask: does this carry naming rights, media rights, digital rights, and/or licensing implications simultaneously? Document all layers, not just the primary one.

7. Step-by-Step Process

How to conduct a physical/rights-based asset audit:

  1. Work through the Asset Audit Walkthrough zone by zone, in order, without skipping ahead.
  2. For each zone, list every distinct physical or rights-based asset present or plausible.
  3. For each asset identified, determine its Rights Layer Stack (naming/media/digital/licensing).
  4. Mark each asset as Currently Sold, Currently Unsold, or Not Yet Formally Identified in the rights holder's existing program.
  5. Cross-reference against Phase 03's rights holder archetype — confirm whether media/broadcast rights are held directly or by a league/conference/association before assuming they're available inventory.
  6. Compile the complete list in the Asset Inventory Log (Section 12).

8. Real Industry Examples

A university that had only ever sold jersey sponsorship and primary stadium signage, but through a systematic audit discovered separately sellable training facility naming rights, gate-specific signage, and a previously unlicensed merchandise category — illustrates the value of the full Asset Audit Walkthrough over a surface-level review limited to "obvious" assets.

A stadium naming rights deal that also carries digital rights, broadcast mention value, and appears on official team merchandise — illustrates the Rights Layer Stack concept directly: a single naming rights sale actually represents several distinct rights bundled together, each with its own value contribution.

A conference-level media rights agreement that limits which broadcast/streaming rights an individual member school can separately sell — illustrates why confirming rights holder archetype (Phase 03) before assuming broadcast/streaming inventory is available is a required audit step, not an afterthought.

9. VYRO Best Practices

  • Never stop an audit after finding the "obvious" assets — the Asset Audit Walkthrough exists specifically to force discovery of the assets a rights holder would have missed on its own.
  • Always document the full Rights Layer Stack for major assets, not just the primary right — this materially changes valuation and packaging downstream.
  • Confirm broadcast/streaming rights ownership before listing them as available inventory — especially for federated rights holders (Phase 03), where these rights may sit at the conference or association level.
  • Mark every asset's current status (sold/unsold/unidentified) clearly — an audit that doesn't distinguish these is not actionable for a sales team.
  • Treat training facility and program-level naming rights as real, distinct line items, not minor footnotes to primary venue naming rights.

10. Common Mistakes

  • Stopping the audit after cataloguing only the most visible, obvious assets (jersey, main stadium signage).
  • Treating signage as one generic line item instead of cataloguing each physical location separately.
  • Assuming a rights holder directly controls broadcast/streaming rights without confirming against its actual archetype and any conference/league/association agreements.
  • Missing the Rights Layer Stack — pricing or packaging a naming rights deal as if it only carries the naming right, ignoring the digital and media exposure that comes with it.
  • Confusing licensing rights with merchandise revenue — these are related but distinct, and conflating them can misrepresent what's actually available to a new brand partner.

11. Professional Tips

  • Physically walk a venue when possible (or use detailed maps, photos, and virtual tours when not) — a desk-only audit reliably misses real assets that only become obvious in person.
  • Interview rights holder staff directly about facilities and programs you might not know exist from public information alone (Phase 04's discovery skills apply directly here).
  • Keep a running personal checklist of asset types discovered across different audits — patterns repeat across similar rights holder types, and a strong personal reference list speeds up future audits significantly.

13. Checklists

Physical/Rights-Based Audit Completion Checklist

  • All seven zones of the Asset Audit Walkthrough covered, none skipped
  • Signage catalogued location by location, not as one generic line item
  • Rights Layer Stack documented for every major asset
  • Broadcast/streaming rights ownership confirmed against rights holder archetype
  • Every asset marked with current status (sold/unsold/unidentified)

14. Worksheets

Worksheet: Zone-by-Zone Practice Audit

Before starting the full homework audit, practice the Asset Audit Walkthrough on a venue or organization you're personally familiar with (a local team, your alma mater, a venue you've attended events at). Identify at least 20 assets across at least five of the seven zones, to build fluency with the method before applying it at full scale.

15. Homework

  1. Complete the Zone-by-Zone Practice Audit worksheet.
  2. Choose one professional sports organization and begin a full Asset Inventory Log, focused specifically on physical and rights-based assets from this lesson (naming rights in all three sub-types, jersey sponsorship, venue signage by location, broadcast rights, streaming rights, licensing, and merchandise). Target at least 75 distinct assets from this lesson's categories alone — this combines with Lesson 6.2's digital/experience/community assets to reach the full 150-asset requirement for Phase 06.
  3. For your ten most significant identified assets, document the full Rights Layer Stack.

Deliverable format: one document containing the Practice Audit, the Asset Inventory Log (75+ physical/rights-based assets), and the ten Rights Layer Stack write-ups.

16. Manager Coaching Notes

Purpose of this lesson in the employee's development: This lesson tests thoroughness and systematic discipline more than conceptual understanding. Managers should specifically check whether the employee's asset list reflects genuine zone-by-zone discovery or stops early after the obvious assets.

Discussion questions to ask in review:

  • "Walk me through your least obvious discovered asset — how did the Asset Audit Walkthrough help you find it?"
  • "Pick one major asset and give me its full Rights Layer Stack — what did you find beyond the primary right?"
  • "Did you confirm broadcast/streaming rights ownership, or assume it? How did you confirm it?"

Expected answers: A proficient employee can point to specific, non-obvious assets discovered through the systematic method (not just the predictable ones), correctly documents multi-layer rights stacks, and can explain how they confirmed (rather than assumed) broadcast/streaming rights ownership.

Coaching tips:

  • If an employee's list stalls well below 75 assets or clusters heavily around only 2–3 zones, send it back with a direct instruction to revisit the skipped zones specifically.
  • If Rights Layer Stacks are consistently single-layer (only the "obvious" right documented), walk through one example together until multi-layer thinking becomes automatic.

Common employee mistakes to watch for:

  • Audits that stop after the obvious assets.
  • Signage treated as one line item instead of location-by-location.
  • Assumed (not confirmed) broadcast/streaming rights ownership.

Evaluation checklist: see Grading Rubric (Section 20).

18. Practical Exercise

Exercise: Timed Zone Audit

Manager assigns one zone (e.g., "Seating Bowl" or "Broadcast & Digital") for an unfamiliar venue or organization. Employee has 20 minutes to identify as many real, distinct assets within that zone as possible, each with a documented Rights Layer Stack.

Evaluation focus: thoroughness within a single zone, and accuracy of Rights Layer Stack documentation.

19. Role-Play Exercise

Scenario: You're interviewing a rights holder's facilities director as part of a Commercial Rights Audit (this connects directly to Phase 04's discovery skills). Your manager plays the facilities director, who mentions venue details only when specifically asked.

Manager's role-play brief: Answer only the specific questions asked, and do not volunteer information about the training facility or any non-primary venue components unless the employee specifically asks about them.

What "good" looks like: The employee asks systematic, zone-by-zone questions (mirroring the Asset Audit Walkthrough) rather than only asking about the primary venue, and successfully surfaces the training facility and other non-obvious assets through deliberate questioning.

23. Key Takeaways

  • Asset inventory development is one of VYRO's most foundational deliverables — valuation and packaging cannot happen without it.
  • The Asset Audit Walkthrough (seven zones, worked in order) systematically prevents the most common audit failure: stopping after only the obvious assets.
  • Most physical assets carry a Rights Layer Stack of multiple commercial rights simultaneously — document all of them, not just the primary one.
  • Broadcast and streaming rights ownership must be confirmed, not assumed, especially for federated rights holders.
  • A thorough, real inventory of 75+ physical/rights-based assets for a single organization is achievable — and expected — when the method is followed completely.
